What We Learned

Reflections on our design thinking journey, the challenges we faced, and how this project transformed our perspective.

Looking Back

Our Design Thinking Journey

From identifying the problem to building a working prototype — here's what the process taught us.

01

Empathize Phase

What we learned: We started thinking about "building a device" but realized we needed to understand PEOPLE first. Interviewing caregivers changed everything — we discovered the real pain wasn't just falling, but the fear of falling and the helplessness of families.

02

Define Phase

What we learned: Narrowing down from "help elderly people" to "detect falls and alert caregivers in under 10 seconds" made our solution focused and achievable. A clear problem statement is half the solution.

03

Ideate Phase

What we learned: The 6-3-5 method showed us that quantity breeds quality. Our best ideas came in rounds 4-6, building on earlier concepts. We also learned that "wild" ideas often contain the seed of breakthrough solutions.

04

Prototype Phase

What we learned: Building is harder than imagining. Our first prototype had 80% false alarm rate! It took 5 iterations to get the multi-stage detection working. Failure is not the opposite of success — it's part of it.

05

Test Phase

What we learned: Testing with real users (our grandparents!) revealed issues we never considered — like the watch being too heavy, buttons too small for arthritic fingers, and the buzzer being frighteningly loud.

Insights

Key Learnings

❤️

Empathy is Everything

We learned that the best solutions don't come from technology — they come from deeply understanding human emotions. Rajesh didn't need a gadget; he needed dignity and peace of mind.

🔄

Iteration is Non-Negotiable

Our first "final" design was terrible. It took 5 prototypes to get something usable. Every "failure" taught us something critical about user needs, physics, and electronics.

🤝

Teamwork Amplifies Ideas

The 6-3-5 method proved that diverse perspectives create better solutions. Ideas we would have dismissed alone became brilliant when combined with teammates' inputs.

💡

Constraints Drive Creativity

Our limited resources and student skills forced us to be creative. We couldn't use expensive sensors — so we wrote better algorithms. Constraints became our advantage.

🎯

Focus Beats Complexity

We initially wanted to build a device that did everything — fall detection, medication reminders, GPS, heart rate, and more. We learned that doing ONE thing exceptionally well is better than doing five things poorly.

🌎

Design for Real People

Testing with elderly users revealed that "intuitive" for us is confusing for them. Large buttons, clear sounds, and simple interfaces aren't optional — they're essential for adoption.

Challenges

Obstacles We Overcame

Technical Challenges

  • 📡

    False Alarm Rate

    Our first prototype triggered on hand waves, door slams, and even sneezing. We solved this with the 5-stage confirmation system.

  • 🔋

    Battery Life

    Initial version lasted only 4 hours. We optimized the code to put the ESP32 in sleep mode between readings, extending life to 18 hours.

  • 📶

    GSM Connectivity

    SIM800L module had inconsistent signal. We added an external antenna and retry logic for SMS sending.

  • 📐

    Casing Design

    Fitting all components into a watch-sized case was nearly impossible. We 3D-printed a custom casing after 3 design iterations.

Process Challenges

  • ⏰

    Time Management

    Balancing this project with academics was tough. We learned to set daily milestones and hold each other accountable.

  • 🗣️

    User Access

    Finding elderly users willing to be interviewed and test prototypes was challenging. We reached out through community centers and family networks.

  • 🧠

    Decision Paralysis

    With 108 ideas, we struggled to choose. The evaluation matrix gave us an objective framework to make confident decisions.

  • 📐

    Scope Creep

    We kept wanting to add more features. Learning to say "no" and focus on the core problem was a valuable lesson.
